Business Concept

The Business Concept is the logical foundation of your entire venture — the bridge between a raw idea ("I want to sell coffee") and every aspect of your business. It is your true north: a blueprint that grounds prototyping, market development, sales, and fundraising.

When your Business Concept is clear, you can compress years of thinking into a 2-line description. If you can't, you're not ready to build.

Why do it?
It challenges you to answer three questions every investor and customer will ask:
Why this?  ·  Why should anyone care?  ·  How do you make money?

Skill Building Exercises

Practice

Practical frameworks to sharpen your venture skills — from crafting your value proposition to delivering an authentic pitch.

Definition

A value proposition is the conversion that results from the combination of a concise problem and a solution that yields an advantage, which in turn, converts to ROI.

50% of the solution is the problem itself. A solution solves a problem when it directly converts from problem to solution — meaning the solution is a derivative of the problem. When the problem is clearly understood, clearly spoken and written, the likelihood of it being true is much higher.

Formulaic Expression

VP = Conversion → [ (Problem × Solution) → Advantage ] → ROI

P × S → A
A → Conversion
Conversion → ROI
Therefore: VP = f(P, S, A, ROI)

In words: A clear problem combined with an effective solution creates an advantage, which drives conversion, ultimately resulting in ROI — the essence of a strong value proposition.

Template 1 — Start Here

via Forbes®

The Forbes® template is the clearest starting point for any value proposition. It forces you to name your customer, their need, and your advantage in a single sentence.

FOR [description of customer] WHO [statement of need], WE DELIVER [unique advantage]

Example: For professional and amateur athletes who want high performance running shoes, we deliver the most advanced performing technology sneakers.

What is a Pitch?

A pitch is a mini speech that moves an audience to action through a mathematically sound value proposition in 60s.

Pitch Mechanics

How to Build a Pitch:

In order to build a pitch, first, you'll need to build a value proposition. Once you have a value proposition, you'll use it to write your pitch script.

A pitch has 3 major parts:

1
Why? — Story-telling/context and problem statement
2
How? — Solution Description
3
What? — Remind the audience who you are and what you offer

Pitch Template

Use the fields below to draft your 60-second pitch. Each part maps directly to one of the 3 pitch components. Complete them in order.

WHY [your story & problem] → HOW [your solution] → WHAT [who you are & what you offer]
1 — Why? (Your story, context & problem statement) 2 — How? (Your solution description) 3 — What? (Who you are & what you offer)
⏱ 60 seconds = ~130 words. Once your draft is complete, time yourself reading it aloud. Cut anything that slows the pace.

The Pitch Script

1. Hook — 15 seconds

Open with a single striking statement or question that makes the problem undeniable.
Template: "Every year, [X] people/businesses face [problem]. Most of them never solve it."

2. Problem — 30 seconds

State the problem clearly, specifically, and emotionally. The audience must feel it.
Template: "[Target customer] struggle with [problem] because [root cause]. This costs them [consequence]."

3. Solution — 30 seconds

Introduce your product or service as the direct answer to the stated problem. No feature lists.
Template: "[Company] is a [category] that helps [customer] [do X] by [unique mechanism]."

4. Market — 20 seconds

Show the scale of the opportunity — TAM, SAM, or SOM depending on your stage.
Template: "The [market] is a $[X]B opportunity, and we are targeting the [$Y]M segment of [specific niche]."

5. Business Model — 20 seconds

Explain simply how you make money. One sentence if possible.
Template: "We charge [who] [how much] for [what] on a [subscription / per-use / licensing] basis."

6. Traction — 20 seconds

Show evidence that the market wants this. Revenue, users, partnerships, LOIs, pilots.
Template: "Since [date] we have [metric: X users / $Y revenue / Z partnerships], growing [rate]."

7. Team — 15 seconds

Why are you and your team uniquely suited to win this? Relevant background only.
Template: "Our team brings [X years] in [domain], having [relevant achievement]."

8. The Ask — 10 seconds

Be specific about what you need and what you will do with it.
Template: "We are raising $[X] to [achieve milestone] within [timeframe]."

⏱ Time yourself. A 3-minute pitch is ~400 words. Practice out loud — not in your head.

Delivery Is Half the Battle

You can have the best pitch script in the room and still lose the meeting. Performance is the bridge between your content and your credibility. These are the fundamentals.

👁
Eye Contact Speak to people, not slides. If you have 3 people in the room, divide your attention equally. Hold eye contact for 3–5 seconds per person — long enough to connect, short enough not to unnerve.
🎙
Voice & Pacing Slow down when making your most important points. Silence is powerful — a 2-second pause after a key statement lands harder than rushing to fill it. Vary your pace; monotone loses the room in 90 seconds.
🧍
Body Language Stand still or move with intention. Pacing randomly signals anxiety. Plant your feet, use open gestures at chest height, and avoid crossing your arms or touching your face.
🔢
Know Your Numbers Cold If you hesitate on your revenue figure, burn rate, or market size, the meeting is over. Rehearse your numbers until they are automatic. Write them on a card. Say them in the shower.
🔁
Repetition Builds Fluency Practice the full pitch out loud at least 20 times before your first real pitch. Record yourself. Watch it back. The discomfort of watching yourself is the fastest feedback loop you have.
🛑
Handle Interruptions Gracefully When an investor interrupts with a question, answer it fully and then say "Coming back to where I was..." Don't lose your thread. Don't skip sections because of interruptions — finish the story.
🏁
Close Deliberately Always end with a clear next step. "I'd love to schedule a follow-up call this week — are you available Thursday or Friday?" Don't end with "Any questions?" and drift off. Own the close.

The Non-Negotiables

These are the rules that separate pitches that get funded from pitches that get polite applause. Break them at your own risk.

1
Lead with the problem, not the product. No one cares about your solution before they feel the problem. Make them feel the pain first.
2
One message per slide. If a slide needs a paragraph to explain it, it needs to be redesigned. One idea. One image. One number.
3
Never read from your slides. Your slides are visual support. You are the pitch. If you are reading, you are not presenting.
4
Pitch outcomes, not features. Investors don't fund features — they fund outcomes. "We save users 3 hours a week" beats "We have an AI-powered dashboard."
5
Your market size must be real. Bottom-up is always more credible than top-down. "We can reach 50,000 customers at $200/year = $10M TAM" beats "$50B global market."
6
Anticipate the top 5 objections. Before every pitch, write down the 5 hardest questions you could be asked. Prepare crisp answers. They will be asked.
7
Always have a specific ask. "We are raising $500K on a $3M cap SAFE to reach X milestone by Y date." Vague asks signal unreadiness.
8
Competition is not "we have none." Every business has competition — direct or indirect. Acknowledging it shows maturity. Denying it destroys credibility.
9
Tell a story, not a presentation. The best pitches follow a narrative arc: world before → disruption → your solution → world after. Make investors feel the journey.
10
Send the deck before the meeting. A prepared investor is a better conversation partner. Never walk in cold if you can help it.

The 10-Slide Framework

A pitch deck is not your business plan — it is a visual argument. Each slide must earn its place. This is the standard sequence used by investors worldwide. Deviate only when you have a compelling reason.

01
CoverCompany name, tagline (one sentence), your name, date. Make it clean. This sets the visual tone.
02
ProblemThe pain you are solving. Make it visceral. Use data, a story, or a quote from a real customer. The investor must feel it.
03
SolutionYour product or service as the direct answer. One screenshot or visual. Avoid feature lists — show the outcome.
04
Market SizeTAM / SAM / SOM. Use bottom-up math. Show you understand which slice you are realistically targeting and why it is large enough to matter.
05
ProductDemo, screenshots, or walkthrough of the core user experience. Show, don't tell. If you have a live product, link to it.
06
Business ModelHow you make money. Pricing, unit economics, LTV vs CAC if you have the data. Keep it to one slide.
07
TractionRevenue, users, growth rate, partnerships, pilot results, LOIs. A chart is worth a thousand bullet points. Show the line going up.
08
CompetitionA 2×2 or feature matrix is fine. Show your differentiation clearly. Never say "no competition." Show why you win.
09
TeamPhotos, names, titles, and the one credential per person that proves you are the team to win this market. Advisors if relevant.
10
The AskAmount raising, use of funds (3–4 buckets), key milestones this round gets you to. Contact info. Make it effortless to say yes.
📐 Design rule: Dark background, large fonts (min 24pt), no more than 30 words per slide. If it takes more than 3 seconds to understand a slide, simplify it.

The Founder Fail: Let's Make Lemonade!

A 10–12 minute one-on-one interview with experienced founders who have built resilience from their journeys.

The show is focused solely on one "fail" the founder chooses to share — a deep reflection for the interviewee, and a source of inspiration for listening founders.

The Purpose

This podcast has a threefold mission:

  1. To create a space for founders and investors to voice their toughest experiences;
  2. To inspire listening founders with real testimonies that show grit and resilience;
  3. To create a normative culture of Trial & Error — where fails and mistakes are not just ok, but are the very source of survival.

Why?

Because Ventures Must Thrive!

PythiaVox's Foundational Premise — Reductio ad Absurdum

90% of startups fail. 75% of funded startups fail. Therefore, funding doesn't build successful startups — resilience is the common denominator among successful startups, including unicorns. So, let's build more resilient startups with programs that promote trial & error!

How?

Each episode is a 10–12 minute one-on-one interview with an experienced founder (minimum 2 years in) whose startup has been through the fire. The interview dissects the founder's experience into real, grounded questions — bringing to the surface the wealth of their experience so that listeners can truly resonate.

Kicking off with Boston Founders — because resilience is a Bostonian Trait!
